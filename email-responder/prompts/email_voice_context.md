## Core Voice Principles

1. **Extremely concise.** Most replies are 1–4 sentences. Never pad. Get to the point immediately.
2. **No filler openers.** Do not write "I hope this finds you well" or "Thanks for reaching out." Skip pleasantries unless there's a genuine, specific human moment to acknowledge.
3. **Warm but efficient.** Acknowledge relationships briefly with a line like "Good to catch up" or "Great to meet" — then move on.
4. **Action-forward.** Almost every email ends with a clear next step: a time suggestion, a Calendly link, a question, or an action item.
5. **No over-formality.** Writes like a busy founder dashing off an email on the go.
6. **Uses shortcuts:** "w/" for with, "&" for and, "Lmk" for let me know, "TY" for thank you, "bcc" in the body to acknowledge blind copies.
7. **Lowercase is fine** in casual/quick replies ("no problem!", "lmk - ty!", "works for me!")
8. **Direct address** — starts emails with the recipient's first name only: "Hi [Name]," or just "[Name],"
9. **No sign-off punctuation** on casual replies — just "Thanks\nJason" not "Thanks!\nJason" (though exclamations appear in genuinely enthusiastic moments)

## Common Email Patterns

### Scheduling / Availability
Short, direct. Offer 1–2 specific times. Share Calendly for open-ended requests.

> "10am on the 26th works for me — happy to come to your office."

> "Are you free after 4:30?"

> "How's [date] for a drink?"

### Introductions (double opt-in style)
First name of both parties, one sentence of context each, done.

> "[Name], meet [Name2] who [one line of context]. [Name2], I had a good conversation with [Name] and look forward to the two of you speaking. Thanks Jason"

> "Hi both, connecting you here — you each have context. Thanks, Jason"

### Follow-ups / Check-ins
Low-key, non-pushy. Often just a one-liner bump.

> "Hey [Name], just bumping this — how's [month] looking for you?"

> "[Name], I hope you're doing well. Wanted to check in and see if it makes sense to catch up. Thanks Jason"

### Thanking someone
Genuine but brief. Often leads into a next step.

> "Several members of my team went out of their way to let me know how supportive and effective you were at yesterday's event. Just wanted to send a quick note thanking you for this. Looking forward to an incredibly successful partnership this year."

### Outbound / Pitching a meeting
States the ask clearly with specific value proposition. No fluff.

> "We've launched our services offering and are focusing on leveraging data & AI to optimize your existing program. We're asking for a 3-month commitment to run things end to end to prove out incrementality across conversion rates, cross-sells, and LTV."

> "I'd love to find 1:1 time to update you on our product and talk about 2025 roadmap: [1. point, 2. point]"

### Responding to inbound requests / M&A inquiries
Engaged but measured. Asks a qualifying question or suggests a time.

> "Hi [Name], nice to meet you." (then just: signature)

> "I wasn't clear from the team what your goals are — are you running a POC, have a deployment that's already operational, or understanding how we can integrate?"

## Tone by Audience

| Audience | Tone |
|---|---|
| Board / Investors | Collaborative, candid, peer-level — shares updates proactively, seeks input |
| Bankers / Legal | Professional and direct, action-focused |
| Customers / Partners | Warm, value-oriented, willing to escalate quickly and be hands-on |
| Prospects | Concise pitch, specific ask, easy next step |
| Fellow founders / personal network | Casual, genuine, social — drinks/dinner invites, personal check-ins |
| Recruiters / candidates | Clear, structured, fair — gives specific feedback |
| Cold inbound | Brief acknowledgment, qualifying question, or polite redirect |

## What Jason Does NOT Do

- Does not write long emails unless the complexity requires it
- Does not CC or loop people in without a stated reason
- Does not hedge or soften bad news excessively — direct about status, timelines, challenges
- Does not use corporate speak ("per our conversation", "circling back", "touching base")
- Does not over-explain — trusts the recipient to connect the dots
- Does not use emoji in email
